# Presentations

Design a deck that serves the speaker, not the slides.

## When to use

- Conference talk, internal all-hands, board meeting, customer pitch event
- Live delivery (15–60 min with Q&A) — not async reading
- The audience will listen more than they read

## When NOT to use

- Pitch deck for investors → use `/business/sales` (different structure, more self-contained)
- Document-style deck meant to be read offline — write a memo instead
- Demo where the product is the star — use screen share + rough narrative, not slides

## Structure

1. **Hook** (1 slide) — a provocation, statistic, or image that earns attention
2. **Problem** (1–2 slides) — what's broken and why it matters
3. **Stakes** (1 slide) — what happens if we don't solve it
4. **Insight / approach** (2–4 slides) — the "aha" that unlocks the solution
5. **Evidence** (3–6 slides) — data, examples, demo, case studies
6. **Ask** (1 slide) — what you want the audience to do
7. **Q&A** — prepare 5 likely questions + strong answers

Total: 10–20 slides for a 20-min talk. Less is more.

## Slide rules

- **One idea per slide** — if two ideas, split into two slides
- **Images > bullets** — charts, diagrams, photos carry meaning faster than text
- **≤ 7 words per line, ≤ 3 lines per slide** — speaker says the rest
- **Speaker notes** — write full script in the notes pane; you memorize the arc, not the words
- **Consistent template** — same font, colors, spacing. Don't let design fight content.

## Narrative shapes

- **Problem → Solution → Proof** — default. Linear, executive-friendly.
- **Three-act** — setup, conflict, resolution. Good for storytelling.
- **Before / After / Bridge** — pain state, outcome state, the bridge that gets you there.
- **Contrarian** — conventional wisdom says X; here's why X is wrong; here's what's actually true.

Pick one shape; don't mix.

## Anti-patterns

- Text-heavy slides the audience reads while tuning out the speaker
- Animation for animation's sake — distracts from the point
- Too many slides for the time slot — slow down, cut
- Logos/quotes/filler that don't advance the argument
- No Q&A prep — the hard question sinks the talk

## Rehearsal

- Read the full script out loud once
- Present to a friendly audience (one person is enough) before the real talk
- Time yourself — cut 10% if you're over budget
- Practice the hook and the ask twice each; everything between can flex

## Output

- Deck (Keynote / PowerPoint / Google Slides / Remark / reveal.js depending on context)
- Speaker notes for each slide
- Q&A prep doc (5 questions, 5 answers)

## Examples

- "Prep a 20-min conference talk on Matchbox for RSA 2026"
- "Draft the monthly Helios progress deck for the Acme Corp leadership"
- "20-slide investor pitch for GeoContext"
- "Internal all-hands deck on the Omarchy migration"
